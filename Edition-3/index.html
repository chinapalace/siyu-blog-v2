Financial Literacy Lesson of the Week - Options on Robinhood, Should you Trade Them?

Last week, a popular discount brokerage service, Robinhood, announced it would soon allow users to buy and sell options on the platform. While we will never provide actionable financial advice in this brief, allow us to provide some words of caution in general; if you don’t understand why “and” was italicized in the last statement from a risk management perspective, you need to brush up on what options actually are, and how they work.

If you would like to learn you should begin with totally free resources such as the foundational “Option Volatility and Pricing” by Sheldon Natenberg (readily available online in PDF version).

Whereas traditional investing involves the buying and selling of equity and bonds, and thus focuses largely on whether the price of the underlying will go up or go down, options can be traded to take advantage of any combination of the numerous factors that go into determining their value, namely price, implied volatility, sensitivity to changes in volatility, sensitivity to price change, time until expiration, the risk free interest rate, etc... As you can see, there is a lot to keep track of when it comes to trading options, in addition to the normal concerns and questions that accompany buying or selling equity and bonds.

Don’t let this discourage you; even as a purely intellectual exercise, learning about options is an enriching experience. We only recommend you take the time to do some of the requisite learning before diving in.

A Deep Dive on Central Banking and its Market Influence

Today’s focus will be on quantitative easing, a term that has been tossed around liberally for the better part of a decade but is often misunderstood.

Post-Great Recession (2008-), central banks (notably the ECB, the Bank of Japan, and the Federal Reserve) tried to stimulate – or to put it bluntly, save – their economies by purchasing enormous sums on government securities (and eventually equity and bonds, too) with the aim of increasing the money supply. They added these quantitative easing measures (QE) while also lowering interest rates (another way of motivating consumers and corporations to take on debt and to spend). Whereas manipulating interest rates has been a staple of any typical Central Bank’s arsenal for multiple decades, this new program of directly purchasing equity, bonds and treasuries on the open market was a new way to try and combat adverse financial conditions domestically and globally.

The first and subsequent iterations of QE initiated in response to the 2008 global financial crises were unique also in scale and timeline. Traditionally, central banks buy and sell short-term securities, with the intended effect of affecting short-term interest rates. QE, on the other hand, saw the purchase of a mammoth amount of both long-term government and non-government securities.  

The goal was clear: keep long term interest rates low for the foreseeable future and get people to spend.

And what has the response been like? Unfettered spending in terms of cash flow into equity, bond, derivative markets, and even other markets such as cryptocurrency when traditional markets got crowded.

This begs a few questions. While QE was arguably very successful in preventing the 2008 financial crises from deepening, why continue to let the pumps flow after 2010, or 2012, or 2014, when economic conditions were already on the uptick?

Keep in mind, that Central Banks traditionally only flexed their muscles in terms of lowering interest rates to spur economic growth or raising interest rates to pump the breaks on an overheating economy. The addition of significant QE as a second vehicle by which to increase cash flow in the economy led to a two-pronged approach. Which returns us to the same question, why keep interest rates near zero for seven years and continue substantial QE at the same time in an improving economy?

 

There can be little debate as to whether or not “easy money” globally has contributed to the unprecedented boom in equity and bond markets (a better question is how much has it contributed). Given that the modern QE model involves directly purchasing equity and bonds, a lot of QE spending translates directly into increases in asset prices. To make this abundantly clear with an example, on any given day in the past decade, a Swiss central banker might have purchased shares in Apple, or purchased debt issued by Netflix, under instruction from his higher-ups. Similarly, continuous near-zero interest rates have spurred a voracious appetite for debt among funds and individual consumers alike. This in turn drives mergers, acquisitions, capital investment, etc.…

Thoughts on what the ultimate consequences of this will be are extraordinarily contentious. There is a line of thought that QE measures can and should be continued globally to bolster economic growth and fight persistently low inflation and deflation. As recently as this week, when the Federal Reserve bumped interest rates up 25 basis points – as part of gradual efforts to normalize rates – there were still two dissenters on the board, one being Neel Kashkari, who voted against the measure citing a preference for a wait-and-see approach when it comes to current U.S. inflation rates.

The key question is, can this easy money fairytale go on forever, or has a monster been created, one that needs to be fed more and more to be sated? 

One thing to think about is what might happen when Central Banks eventually have to unwind their balance sheets and sell back assets they have acquired since 2008. It’s certainly impractical to imagine them owning equity and bonds forever (or, in the case of Japan’s Central Bank, owning 74% of ETFs on the market). Whenever this process begins, it will automatically turn trillions of dollars of past buying pressure into future selling pressure, and this could have a predictable effect on markets.

Similarly, insofar as QE has potentially created illusory equity valuations (driven prices for stocks up in layman’s terms), the two favorite control-assertion tactics of the Central Bank could actually counteract each other’s effectiveness, as these inflated asset price levels could speed up interest rate hikes in an economy that might not be as fortified as we might think.

This would validate part of Kashkari’s aforementioned dissent, but more importantly it would mean that Central Banks have lost their tight control of how their measures affect the economy. This would be a dire scenario, and Central Bankers themselves, such as Dallas Fed President Robert Kaplan, have commented on it:

“As a central banker, I want to be vigilant to imbalances and distortions that can build as a result of accommodative monetary policy. I have argued that monetary policy accommodation is not ‘free’ – there are costs to accommodation in the form of distortions and imbalances in consumer decisions as well as in investing, hiring and other business decisions. More specifically, experience suggests that the greater the overshoot of full employment, the more difficult it is to unwind imbalances when growth ultimately slows – as it certainly must.” (Credit is due here)

But it’s also possible that asset valuations are more appropriate than they seem.  

It’s also possible that U.S. GDP is growing at a solid 3% clip as advertised and that fundamentals are strong. It’s also possible that Central Bankers are unduly concerned when they themselves voice all of the concerns we delineated above. It’s just hard to tell. Especially considering that a total of $12T + of asset purchases have been conveniently injected into global markets since 2008.  

Perhaps the biggest thing to pay attention to in 2018 will be when (and if at all) the Federal Reserve starts unwinding their balance sheet. As you can see below, there hasn’t been much progress on this end since 2014, when Janet Yellen first took office.
