A Glance at Futures

While the typical market watcher may not be overtly interested in commodities, it’s worth turning our eye to the happenings in a market that, more than any other, deals in tangible goods. Commodity itself just means a raw material. Things we use every day like wheat, cocoa, and metals that are readily used in our technology are actively traded in the commodities markets.

2017s strongest performing commodities included Palladium, an elemental metal primarily used in catalytic converters, which control emissions and exhaust in automobiles. The rise in Palladium’s value can be attributed at least in part to its integral role in electric car production; commodity futures for Palladium saw an appreciation of more than 50% in 2017.

Time for some basic financial education; a future, which is the primary financial instrument used to trade commodities, is simply a contract made between a buyer and seller to exchange a fixed quantity of the underlying – in this case the commodity, but with respect to other types of future contracts, the underlying can be shares of the S&P 500 as well – at a fixed price, on a fixed future date.

Futures gained popularity as a tool for farmers to lock in prices for their crops, rather than subjecting themselves to potential market swings that could reduce the value of their yet-to-be-had harvest. They have been used in this capacity as early as the 17th century, if not earlier. When people discuss the tulip bulb mania in 1636 and compare its lessons regarding financial bubbles to cryptocurrencies, the bubble they are discussing occurred largely in futures contracts for tulip bulbs, not with respect to the physical tulip bulbs themselves. The use of futures contracts in agricultural is still very much a common practice, but institutional and retail investors alike also use futures as a method of speculating.

Big losers in the futures market last year included products often sourced from South America, including coffee, cocoa, sugar, and others. Fears of inclement weather had originally sent prices higher earlier in the year, but supply eventually outweighed said fears. Price action in futures often moves accordingly, investors and producers try to forecast future supply and demand. If supply appears to be robust, prices will fall. Similarly, weather, or even diseases and fungi such as coffee rust can threaten supply, and lead prices to climb.

First Day of Trading

You’re not the only one with a New Year’s Resolution—the markets have high hopes heaped on them as they try to keep their mojo working in 2018.

Markets opened up again everywhere yesterday. US equities looked poised for another run of gains, as the NASDAQ managed a full 1.5% while the S&P and the Dow ticked off double digit basis point rallies. In many ways, January 2nd was a neat summary of 2017 in markets; the VIX (which we have discussed previously) got crushed in orderly fashion as market wide gains reduced volatility (and fear of potential new year, new sentiment changes) in options markets. The Dow - which we will remind you is an index comprised only of 30 big, mostly recognizable companies for the average consumer - now sits only a stone’s throw away from the psychological 25,000 level, which we may even hit today.

Not too long ago we were still heralding in Dow 20k, and we now contemplate this next threshold! However, before moving forward with our new year, it’s worthwhile to reflect on just how unique 2017 was. Outstanding performance wasn’t limited to the Dow’s slice of the market; the S&P 500 did not have a single negative month last year, a feat that has never before happened according to the Wall Street Journal.

We don’t know how much longer the rally will continue; what we do know however is that this rally has not been limited to the “developed” markets of the United States, Europe, or Japan. Rather, it truly has been a global phenomenon. Emerging markets (EM) — from the Czech Republic to Argentina to Turkey — also partook in the fun of 2017, and they have performed especially strong heading into 2018. In sum, emerging markets as a whole actually outperformed U.S. markets in 2018.

It will be interesting to see if divergences in the performance of developed and emerging markets widen. But for now, the start of 2018 has provided nothing out of the ordinary: a continuation of markets that have seen little but green, new highs, compressed volatility, and further forecasts of the same stretching out into the horizon, with synchronized global economic growth (probably the buzz word you will hear the most the next few months) offering an underpinning explanation.

2018 hasn’t thrown us a surprise yet (at least not in the market arena). We’re waiting.
