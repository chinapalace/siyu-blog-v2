Friends,

Happy Wednesday. It’s a big day, not only because you get to read another brief from us, but also because the Fed will report its minutes (at 2 p.m. eastern) from its last meeting at the end of January. We know this excites you as much as it excites us. In any case, one item on their agenda will indubitably be the national deficit. As promised last week, we are going to do an in-depth discussion of this topic today.

United States sovereign debt (for instance, a US treasury bond) is among the most sought after and reliable investments in the world. Investors, both on an individual and institutional level and from a domestic and international perspective, have been buying US treasuries in droves for decades.

At current, China is the largest foreign holder of US treasuries; mainland China held $1.2 trillion of US debt in October 2017. Part of China’s appetite for US debt stems from their trade surplus with the US. (A trade surplus means that a country exports more goods than it imports. Or, it sends out more goods and services to other countries than it receives.)

Per Bloomberg, “Since China has a large trade surplus with the U.S., it has excess dollars -- which the government plows into U.S. Treasuries.”  In total, foreign entities own over $6 trillion of US treasuries.

Another intuitive reason US debt is so desirable is its “risk-free” status. Given its backing by the US government, the likelihood of a default on US treasuries is considered almost zero. Are US government bonds actually risk free? No, and more on this later. But investors consider them as close to risk-free as possible in practice.

This explains why yields for treasuries are frequently so low; they only rise significantly in periods when investors are holding more cash or are moving money out of debt into other asset classes. (Otherwise, US treasuries almost always comprise some portion of a modern portfolio, which, per modern portfolio theory, is always comprised of some mix of risky assets and risk-free assets.)

For instance, before the 2008-2009 financial crisis, the 3-year US treasury was yielding close to 3.5%, as investors abandoned defensive bonds and plowed money into the equity and housing market. Conversely, during periods of conservative investment (see 2012-2013 on the chart below), the 3-year treasury was in high demand, yielding less than 1%.

Little refresher: as a bond (e.g., the 3-year US treasury) sees increased demand, its price rises (simple supply and demand), which drives the yield downwards. Price and yield in bonds move inversely.

(Not to delve too far into esotericism here, but treasury yields and prices are determined in “Dutch” auctions. All you really need to know about this is that there are generally far more bids for treasuries than there are treasuries on sale. The highest bids are filled first, followed by the next highest, etc. until all treasuries have been sold. When we talk about treasury yields moving higher, it indicates that demand in treasury auctions is waning, reducing the prices at which bids are filled.)

To tie some previous pieces together here, let’s remember that bond yields are interpreted as being comprised of inflation plus a risk premium. With an inflation rate of approximately 2% at current, and a 3-year yield around 2.5%, the astute reader will notice that most of the treasury yield is just inflation.

The risk premium in treasuries, aka the default risk of the United States government, can be loosely evaluated in TIPS (Treasury Inflation Protected Securities), as these are treasuries that are also indexed to an inflation index, thereby stripping out the inflation yield. As you might imagine, yields on TIPS are quite low. Given that these bonds compensate for US default risk premium alone, they typically yield well below 1%.

Phew. Let’s pause for a second. Here’s the picture we have so far; US debt is considered very safe. It is very sought after.

As a nation, our relationship with our debtholders, as you can imagine from the above set-up, is very strong. The US always makes its interest payments to its creditors (synonymous with debtholder); this is the crucial piece of the relationship we are exploring.

As long as foreign and domestic investors are being paid on time per the covenants of the debt they hold, they’re happy and they may (hopefully) continue to buy more US debt in the future. If this relationship were to break down however, as it pertains to US debt, we and the world together could face a dire scenario.

This is why the national deficit is not some blowsy number we can inflate recklessly ad infinitum.

In case you are actually a nice, happy ostrich with your head nestled in the sand, we’ll mention that at current, the US national deficit is increasing. It has been in aggregate for decades. The last time it decreased on an annual basis was during the Clinton Administration. This chart gives some color to the US Debt as a percentage of GDP. There are a few times when total debt is greater than our GDP. Now is one of those times.

Simply put, we owe more in total outstanding debt than we make in a given year. Even if we could contribute 100% of our annual GDP to paying off outstanding debt (this would in no way be a good use of capital even if feasible), there would still be some debt on the other side of that transaction.

 

In fact, the US’ current debt/GDP is 105%. Japan is even more extreme -- around 250%, where more conservative countries like Germany and South Korea have levels of 68% and 39%, respectively.

But, as noted above, as long as we are paying the interest on our debt to our debtholders, things work out.

There are a number of variables we alluded to last week that could jeopardize our future ability to finance our national debt. Paying interest on existing outstanding debt is in part predicated on being able to issue new debt at comparable costs. This is why a scenario in which, 10 or 20 years down the line, the US is no longer the global preferred issuer of debt is problematic.

If, for instance, China overtakes the US as the premier global economic power, and their debt becomes safer in the eyes of investors than that of the US as a consequence, investors would require more compensation for buying US debt rather than Chinese debt. This would increase the future interest payments on US sovereign debt, as yields on treasuries would climb.

In the long haul, this represents one of a few escalation scenarios in which the deficit would become a more drastic issue, as both demand for US debt coupled with higher financing costs would undermine the integrity of the capital structure within which our nation currently operates.

Let’s have a look at a country dealing with a doomsday debt scenario.

The Venezuelan economy has always been dependent on oil. The state owned petroleum company, Petroleos de Venezuela (PDVSA) has seen production decline from 3.4 million barrels of oil per day in 1998 to 1.7 million barrels as of recent. This halving of production was masked when oil peaked around $150 per barrel in 2008, but when oil prices fell below $40 in recent years, the effect on the economy was predictable (historical crude oil prices are shown below).

(source)

Not only is there an extraordinary humanitarian crisis at foot in Venezuela, but Venezuela has become the most indebted country in the world as a consequence of the above outlined economic factors. This means they have few options when it comes to turning the ship around.

Given the dire state of the Venezuelan economy, there is zero international demand for new Venezuelan debt, and thus Venezuela can’t issue new debt to finance their existing obligations. They’ve turned instead to printing currency, but they have had to do so at a clip that has the nation spiraling towards hyperinflation. (Concept check: rapidly “printing” currency -- or adding new bills to the money supply -- causes the value of each bill to decrease. The cost of goods increases in lockstep. Hyperinflation is the extreme result.) In 2017, the minimum wage in Venezuela was insufficient to meet “even 12% of an individual’s basic food needs.”

Part of Venezuela’s debt situation derives from the fact that public spending remained high as long as oil prices did. Despite declining oil production, high prices intoxicated policy makers into increasing the public-sector deficit when they should have been stashing cash for an oil price drawdown scenario. This ties into the discussion of the US deficit at current we will now begin.

Without commenting on the role political mismanagement played in ruining the economy of a country with some of the world’s most lucrative oil reserves, we simply want to note how external economic factors can threaten a country’s ability to make good on its external debt. This is clearly an example at the far reaches of logical extremes.

So what?

The federal budget proposal proposed by the White House puts the country at risk of increasing the deficit even further in the coming decade. We addressed some of the specifics last week, but it’s worthwhile to touch upon them again. Here’s a clip from last week:

Per the New York Times, the plan increases the deficit by $1 trillion — yes trillion with a T not B — over the coming decade. (The size of this increase is predicated in part on recent tax cuts and proposed spending programs, i.e., the wall and military spending).

The current US debt is around $20T, so a $1T increase is not an insignificant amount (5%). We also noted last week that the assumptions of the plan are ambitious. For instance, the budget plan assumes annual 3% GDP growth over the next decade, which is flat-out high considering our position that must be if not in the middle, then towards the later stage of a historically long economic expansion period / bull market. Reference the below chart to begin to form your own opinion about whether or not we can maintain high levels of growth for another ten years.

All of the above being said, it is plausible that the deficit could increase by considerably more than a $1T during the coming decade. The US has not always handled fiscal and monetary policy as it has in the last year. Historically, peaks in the deficit came during war time, with reductions coming during the following peace times. The Reagan Administration was the first to really heighten up the deficit during times of peace (or not total war). The trend has mostly continued.

The risks of a deficit run afoul are huge. Insofar as we can already see debt catastrophes on smaller scales (see Illinois’ unfunded pension liabilities), we don’t want to live in a country where these scenarios arise on a macro scale.

A closing question to ponder is whether or not markets can (and currently are) accurately pricing in future deficit drags on the economy. We’ll spend some time thinking about this and will report back in later briefs.

The music of cheap debt and deficit expansion must stop eventually, even if it starts to slow incrementally.

Options Bonanza Continued

The below chart, combined with an understanding of the Greeks we mentioned last week, will give you a schema to interpret how changes in an underlying asset would affect the value of an option position.

  1. If you are long a call option, it means you have bought the call option, and unless you’re hedging a separate short position, you expect the price of the underlying to increase, the volatility of the underlying to increase, or the risk free interest rate to increase (or some combination of the above)
  2. If you are long a put option, it means you have bought the put option, and unless you’re hedging a separate long position, you expect the price of the underlying to decrease, the volatility of the underlying to increase, or the risk free interest rate to decrease (or some combination of the above)
  3. If you are short a call option, it means you have sold the call option, and unless you’re hedging a separate long position, you expect the price of the underlying to decrease, the volatility of the underlying to decrease, or the risk free interest rate to decrease.
  4. If you are short a put option, it means you have sold the put option, and unless you’re hedging a separate short position, you expect the price of the underlying to increases, the volatility of the underlying to decrease, or the risk free rate to increase.

Being short options without any other positions in the underlying assets is one of the riskiest positions to hold as an investor in any market. In the case of being short calls, you are exposed to unlimited risk, as the price of the underlying could theoretically increase to infinity. In the case of being short puts, your risk is only limited by the zero bound on the underlying. Here’s a maximum risk reward profile for selling short puts on the S&P 500 (this is dated by about a week):

These trades are far from uncommon. Traders who sell options in this fashion are accepting a risk reward profile that is highly asymmetrical; the probability of small profits is very high, with a very low probability of commensurately large losses. Remember, an options trader would only sell short $SPX puts if he or she thinks market estimates of volatility are exaggerated, or if he or she is confident the index itself will trade flat or increase. It was easy to get anesthetized to market risk in the bull run of 2017, but a lot of traders got caught out in trades likes this in the volatile market environment two weeks ago.

Things didn’t go so well for a mutual fund (ironically named the LJM Preservation and Growth Fund) two weeks ago, as the fund was short put options on S&P 500 futures. The fund lost 80% of its value over the course of last week’s sell off. That’s a nightmare scenario for retail investors who are accustomed to mutual fund risk not being exceptionally high.

Back to options: note that time decay (the inevitable decrease in time to expiration) will decrease the value of all long positions and increase the value of all short positions. Think of it this way; an investor who is long a call will always prefer that his or her option has more rather than less time to expiration. Greater time to expiration provides more time for the option to appreciate in value (for a speculator) or provides longer insurance coverage (for a hedger). Sellers of options take the opposite position of the longs, and the calculated risk they take is based on the fact that the option will eventually expire.

Lastly, it is worth noting again that Rho is the least influential Greek. Option prices are least sensitive to interest rate changes. It is unlikely anyone would ever trade options based strictly on an interest rate prediction. You could trade futures contracts on treasuries to achieve far greater and more efficient leverage in an interest rate trade.

When we discuss the risk-free-interest rate for options, it is typical to use a benchmark figure; tying a week’s worth of content together, the one-year US treasury rate is frequently used.
