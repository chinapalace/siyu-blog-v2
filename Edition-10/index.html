Good morning friends,

What a week. We have so much to talk about. We are so excited. Let’s dive straight in, with the below preface outlining thoughts on the past few days:

“…It's a reminder that the real danger to a portfolio is not a bear market -- we recover from those quite nicely as a nation -- it's the delirium that happens when a bull market gets totally out of control…”

Per the words of another technical analyst we follow, “Holy shit balls.” Monday’s market sell off was the most volatile move in major US indices in the last six and a half years. For many of us, that constitutes the first such event in our lives as conscious capital market participants. We have the extraordinarily difficult task of trying to digest all of the important takeaways from this week in one brief. We’ll certainly try. Oh, remember how we noted last week that we were in the 2nd longest spell of without a 5% pullback in equities? Sorry for jinxing it.

Fortunately, we are more confident than ever in the worth of our work so far, as countless topics we have discussed over the first ten weeks have taken center stage in this entirely new market environment. For our new followers, if topics in the following are unfamiliar, feel free to reach out and we will gladly send you older editions of this brief that cover topics like the VIX in greater depth.

Monday was a clear and substantial breakout from the multi-year trend of volatility compression that accompanied gains across all indices from the end of 2016 through the beginning of this year. As we will discuss shortly, this single move in the VIX also completely undermined the overall viability of a potentially trillion dollar market that was, is, and continues to be short (betting against) volatility.


How did this happen?

As mid-day selling turned panicky on Monday, and the Dow shed multiple percentage points in a fifteen minute span, active traders and algorithms alike piled into options as a hedge against a continued drawdown in the markets. Options can be used to bet against stocks, as we’ve discussed previously. When demand for options picks up, it increases option prices and the implied volatility of options, as implied volatility is an input in the option pricing formula per the Black-Scholes model. The VIX measures the broader, average implied volatility of options on the S&P 500 index (a topic we have also discussed at length previously). As purchasing of and hedging with options increases rapidly, so too does the VIX reading.

So what does a VIX reading of ~ 38 (approximate highs recorded Monday) really mean? Here’s a sexy party trick for you - if you take the VIX reading and divide by the square root of 12 (~ 3.46), you have the expected percentage move for the next 30-day trading period in the S&P 500. By this quick logic, a VIX reading of 34.6+ means markets are pricing in a 10+ % move over the next 30 days in the S&P 500.

On Tuesday, the VIX soared even higher as wild swings characterized the early morning trading session. It printed a reading of 50.30 at its highest, before sinking back into the 30s. By god, that’s some volatility. We missed you, VIX.

Other Root Causes of This?

Knowing what else was at work here is hard, but it’s reasonable to assume the Fed’s meeting last week may have spooked investors as they fear tighter monetary policy moving forward will end the global stock and bond market party sooner than expected. Why does the Fed need to keep hiking rates? Inflation should finally be showing up to crash the aforementioned party, for one. Other analysts point to the turmoil regarding the release of the FBI memo as a potential catalyst.


The End of $XIV and Possibly the Entire Short-Volatility Trade
Remember when we discussed the short volatility trade in Week 6? Let us refresh your memory. Essentially, for the past seven years, more and more cash has been jammed into financial products that profit from a systematic decline in market volatility as measured by the VIX. These products made money specifically by harvesting the roll-yield and risk premium in medium to long term VIX futures. As a brief synopsis, roll-yield is the phenomenon that arises by virtue of the VIX futures curve’s general state of contango, which refers to the fact that futures with a later expiration tend to trade with a risk premium which leads their price to be above the spot (actual) price of the VIX. As future expiration dates draw nearer, the value of the corresponding futures contracts converge down to the spot price as long as the VIX doesn’t increase, yielding a return for the financial products that short (bet against) these contracts.
But what happens when the VIX explodes on Monday, as outlined above? Well, when we first wrote about the highly popular short VIX product $XIV, we noted that a surge in the VIX could cause a drawdown of 80-90% in the product. We were exactly right about this. $XIV fell from $112 to sub $20 in after-hours trading on Monday. Here’s a chart for $XIV on Monday after-hours:

(Chart credit here)


What we didn’t know, and what individual  investors all over unfortunately neglected to read, was a clause in the contract specifications for $XIV which stated that a 80% drawdown in the product grants its sponsor the right to liquidate its assets.
Disclaimer: opinions rather than facts follow. Why does this matter? For one, as risky as investors should have known this trade to be, this in-contract functionality is ridiculous; it wipes out investment entirely for a scenario that was far from impossible. And the worst part is that these investments will stay wiped out, even if volatility is back down over the coming weeks. For an analogy, owning $XIV could be likened to selling fire insurance. You reap small profits repeatedly for a while. Then, if a big fire happens, you lose a lot. But the 80% liquidation clause was a functional stipulation that once the first big fire happened, you could never sell insurance again. We’re not actuaries, but we’re pretty positive that’s not typical of the insurance markets, and that such functionally reduces expected value long-term for this type of strategy to zero. In sum, as the VIX stabilizes, the value of $XIV should have stabilized too. But it won’t. It’s gone. Dead. Kaput.

$XIV in particular is another classic example of a major bank selling a disastrous product (albeit, it was a documented clause) to unsuspecting retail investors who were lured in by the promise of seven years of profit.

Incidentally, one of the inventors of the VIX, which these disastrous products tracked, came out today, openly wondering why any such products exist in the first place. Here’s a brief excerpt:

“In my wildest imagination I don’t know why these products exist. Who do they benefit? No one, except if someone wants to gamble -– then, OK, just go gamble…”


Reasons Not to Panic

You might be wondering, should I be scared? Should I sell all my stocks? Should I buy an AR-15 and stock up on penicillin and tobacco? Probably not. The economic fundamentals driving the market really have not changed that much in the last week. The usual suspects — GDP growth, employment, wage growth — remain strong, and other leading indicators such as the real estate market aren’t acting as the canary-in-the-coal-mine, yet.

We touched on the fact that interest rate concerns may have catalyzed the sell-off, but theoretically markets should be able to price this in over the coming weeks without triggering massive movements. Obviously, theory and fundamentals don’t always coincide with reality.

Also, forget headlines such as “Largest Point Drop Ever in the Dow.” They’re pretty useless insofar as they don’t address percentage change. Of course point drops in indices are higher when those same indices were at all-time highs beforehand. We’ve previously addressed the fecklessness of these sorts of headlines in the past. Over the past few years, the headlines have read something like “Largest DOW Daily Increase Ever,” but now the headlines are being applied to the downside. Think in percentages, not absolute points.


Reasons to Panic a Little Bit

It’s very difficult to understand how wide-spread the tentacles of the short volatility trade stretched, and how destructive the fall-out from the liquidation of products like $XIV will be.

As noted previously, even non-profit institutions like Harvard were in on this trade. Everyone wanted in on the gravy train. Who was exposed, and how exposed, should become more clear in the coming days. If the water level retreats, we’ll see some players were swimming naked. One estimate for total value tied up in short volatility trades comes in a $2 trillion!

Additionally, sectors that normally act as safe-havens in times of market turmoil did not see cash inflows on Monday. For instance, even during recessions, people are still going to buy consumer staples. What Else to Look For
A market rally on Tuesday — which is precisely what we got! We need to see stabilization in Treasury demand to stop bleeding in equities overall. Treasuries will be auctioned twice more this week – on Wednesday and Friday – and these auctions determine prices and yields. Watch these closely; stabilization or declines in yields would likely continue to buffer equity markets.

Beyond this, let’s pay attention to other traditional indicators of economic health. The Dow Jones Transportation Average is used as a proxy for overall economic health, and divergences in its performance from the Dow Jones Industrial Average often signal issues.

So far the two are still moving in tandem over this past week of turmoil:

Tuesday’s volatility and market swings were simply unreal. We were both glued to our screens and it’s safe to say we’ll be doing little else in the coming days but continuing to watch and trade this new market environment. Wednesday (today) will be a pivotal trading day as we wait to see whether the rally continues or collapses under its own weight. The aforementioned Treasury auction will give more insight to things. In the interim, enjoy the unprecedented ride! This is where the big guns come out to play.
