van Osdol’s-VIX Deep Dive

In the second Edition of this brief, we promised that we would delve into the specifics of how the VIX works at a later date. Today is that day. Without further ado, we now begin the perhaps-patentable van Osdol-VIX deep dive.

The VIX has gained attention in a fashion negatively correlated with the values it has been printing, as it has systematically sunk to historical lows in the last twelve months. But what exactly are we talking about when we talk about the VIX, it’s derivative products, and its implications for the broader market?

The VIX was introduced in 1993 by the Chicago Board Options Exchange (the CBOE). In its current form, the VIX measures the aggregate expected volatility across S&P 500 options ($SPX options). To get a slightly more intimate understanding of what exactly this means, lets recall how options are valued using the Black Scholes method. Without outlining the entire formula, the value of an option depends, in part, on implied volatility, a variable in the formula. All other variables being held constant, when implied volatility for an underlying asset increases, options for that asset increase in price.

By virtue of the fact that all options trading on the open market trade for specific prices, and that all other variables in the Black Scholes calculations – such as stock price and time to expiration – are readily available or discoverable, the implied volatility for traded options can be calculated at any given time. This should make the following statement somewhat more clear: The VIX continuously returns a reading that tracks (we are not implying that this is exactly how it functions here) implied volatility across S&P 500 options at a given point in time. As long as S&P 500 options are changing in price in aggregate, the value of the VIX will change. If the implied volatility of S&P 500 options increases (decreases), the VIX will increase (decrease).

Now we must continue by stating that even the above description is an oversimplification of how the VIX is calculated. But for our purposes it will suffice. Full contract specifications for the VIX are provided by the CBOE, and can be found here, and further reading on VIX nuances can be found in the news today.

Another important characteristic of the VIX is that it tends to lie ‘dormant’ for stretches of time when market activity is relatively steady. In volcano-esque fashion however, the VIX spikes when the market experiences significant pull-backs, or at the beginning of new bear markets and economic downturns. It is also wrong to characterize spikes in the VIX as necessarily being short lived. In the above chart, you can see that for the entire second half of 2008 and first half of 2009, VIX readings were considerably high from a historical perspective. These were times when the market regularly oscillated multiple percentage points daily. For reference, the S&P 500 is currently continuing its longest ever streak without a 3% pullback.

Now – as also advertised in previous editions of this brief – we will delve into how derivative VIX products make money (and a lot of it), even when the VIX is only falling slightly, or isn’t moving much at all. In order to approach this discussion with some facility, you should leverage our brief overview of futures from last week (edition 5). One of 2017s most successful short-volatility products was $XIV, or the Velocity Shares (the company that provides the product) Daily Inverse VIX ST ETN. Let’s dissect the name of this product; it provides investors with an exchange traded note (aka purchasable on any brokerage) by which they can “inverse” (sell short) short-term (hence the ST in the title) VIX futures. Note that this product tracks futures, not the index itself. And this is key to how $XIV minted money for investors in 2017.

As discussed last week, futures contracts specify the exchange of a specified quantity of something at a specific date, for a specific price. There are futures designed to track the VIX index which are settled in cash, there is no expectation that traders will have to physically deliver or receive the VIX given that unlike beef or corn, it isn’t a physical good. In any case, there are VIX futures with expiration dates ranging from “weeklies” to multiple years from now. One important thing to understand about the normal state of the VIX futures market is that longer-dated futures are normally more expensive than shorter-dated futures. By purchasing long-dated VIX futures, an investor could be speculating on a future spike in the VIX, or paying a premium to hedge. Acknowledging as we did earlier that changes in volatility are typically negatively correlated with changes in market prices, going long volatility can also be seen as a way of hedging against market decline.

This makes intuitive sense in today’s market conditions; given that market volatility is very low historically and market performance is strong, people are willing to pay for longer term protection against a rise in the VIX. The longer the protection holds the better. This scenario, in which futures becomes more expensive as days to settlement increase is referred to as a futures curve that is in contango.

Futures are not always in contango, and “normal” futures curve conditions vary by what commodity or index the futures in question track. If the VIX were to spike up to 50 tomorrow from < 10 today, the VIX futures curve would likely invert. The spike in the VIX would lead to significant short-term repositioning, as investors clamored for VIX protection in what would indubitably be a corrective market environment. Long-dated VIX futures would be less attractive suddenly than short-dated ones, as the VIX - while producing high readings in the short term - could very well fall again over a medium or longer term time frame. This scenario in which futures become less expensive as days to settlement increase is called backwardation.

(The previous two charts stem from the paper “Easy Volatility Investing” by Tony Cooper, February 2013)

So how does $XIV make money shorting short-term VIX futures? The answer is that it does only as long as the VIX futures are in contango (first chart with positive, asymptotic slope). Baring a significant spike in the VIX, short-term VIX futures will constantly be falling in value. This is a concept called roll yield, by which futures in contango must roll down to converge with the current value (spot price) of the index or commodity, lest there be an arbitrage opportunity on the day of settlement. Since $XIV shorts the two VIX contracts with the nearest settlement dates (it takes two to contango, get it, haha2), we can appreciate why the product has been making money even with little movement in the VIX itself.

VIX futures are currently in contango, and their roll yield can be calculated as the difference between the average price of the short-term futures and the spot price of the VIX; as long as the VIX is flat or moves lower, the expiring futures roll down to the spot price and shorting them results in a gain commensurate with the value of the roll yield.

For quick reference, roll yields in VIX futures can exceed 1% daily. Simply holding $XIV since February of 2016 would have returned upwards of 700%. But we would be remiss if we didn’t mention that back testing for a 2008-2009 scenario - where the VIX recorded an all-time intraday high of 89.53 - shows that 80-90% wipeouts in these types of short-VIX products are also possible on a day to day basis.
