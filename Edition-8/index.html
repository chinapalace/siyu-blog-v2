Interest Rates; Pivotal Prices in the Market

As we forage ahead with our weekly briefs, we will at times return to more elementary conversations as they inform esoteric topics we wish to touch on in the future. Last week we took a step back to discuss bonds at length, which we hope was fruitful. Today we will engage similarly in a discussion of interest rates, which will hopefully add to a burgeoning platform of knowledge we can leverage moving forward.

What is an interest rate? Your first instinct may be to think of your credit card, or any sort of loan you have taken out in the past. If you carry a balance on your credit card, or have student loans for instance, you pay a certain interest rate on that outstanding debt. This interest rate is generally quite high for credit card debt. Fortunately, for student loan debt, it is usually much lower. You may not know exactly what rates you pay, but typically they will be fixed, and compound at regular intervals, perhaps monthly or annually.

Today we wish to discuss why interest rates are among the most important prices in capital markets. We say prices because interest rates represent the cost of borrowing, the cost of spending, and the opportunity cost of capital all at once.

To dig into this in greater detail, let’s think of an interest rate as the required rate of return for an investment. This is pivotal. If you had the opportunity to lend your money out at 7% compounded annually, any other investment with a lower rate of return would be unsuitable. In this manner interest rates become hurdles by which we judge other potential investments. In other words, they become measures of opportunity cost for anyone who can lend their money out at said rate. Equally, this opportunity cost is also important when we evaluate the time value of money, or the cost of current consumption as opposed to saving or investment.

You may have also heard the term discounted cash flows thrown around the financial arena before. Let’s think of a tangible example. When investors analyze the relative attractiveness of an investment, such as a fixed-income vehicle like a bond that will pay out specified amounts of cash at future dates, these future cash flows must be discounted back to their present value by leveraging the discount rate in order to appropriately calculate yield. This ties back into the opportunity cost comparison we related earlier; for an investment to be attractive, it must promise a greater return than could be achieved by simply lending out the money. If the investment and projected cash flows span multiple years, then annual interest rates would compound multiple times over the life of the investment, and we divide the future cash flows by this compounded discount factor to arrive at the present value of the investment.

All fundamental valuation of equity, bonds, and of companies themselves depends on interest rates (interchangeable referred to as discount rates). When interest rates are low, as they have been since the financial crisis, asset prices are discounted with said lower rates. Lower rates yield higher valuations and higher asset prices. Similarly, low interest rates indicate lower costs of debt financing for entities looking to invest in new projects, or for companies looking to merge together or acquire each. Corporate transactions aren’t limited to situations when the purchasing party has 100% of the capital to make the acquisition; debt, especially when cheap, is often used to meet the purchase price. (See Leveraged Buyouts, or LBOs).

Hopefully you are beginning to appreciate the pervasiveness of interest rates in the environment of capital markets.

So where do interest rates come from? Are they magically set? The answer is that Central Banks and general economic conditions play important roles in setting rates.

The Federal Reserve sets targets for one interest rate explicitly, namely the Federal funds rate. This is the interest rate at which banks and other depository institutions lend money to each other overnight. Banks have reserve requirements they must maintain (hint: they don’t just hold 100% of your savings in cash, they lend or invest all but a fraction of it), which explains one potential impetus for borrowing overnight. While overnight lending rates are negotiated between banks, the Federal Reserve uses open market operations to keep the observed rate in line with target. If banks are husbanding cash and are adverse to overnight lending, as was the case (critically so) during the financial crisis, the Fed will increase the supply of money in the US economy (by purchasing US treasuries for instance) to incentivize greater lending by introducing more cash into banks’ balance sheets. Especially note the absolute flat line in rates since the financial crisis. We have had a decade of a near zero federal funds rate, which trickled down throughout the economy and resulted in cheap debt. Now, with economic growth strong, and market conditions positively exuberant, it’s clear that the goal of stimulating investment and growth via low rates has worked, even if it took longer than anticipated. The question now becomes, have we gone too far?

Let’s have some weigh in from one of the leading authorities on the interest rate topic. James Grant edits “Grant’s Interest Rate Observer” and outlined the following thoughts on the marriage between our current low rate environment and current economic conditions:

“We live in a hall of mirrors which our central banks have made for us and that hall of mirrors distorts perceptions. That, I think is a big problem…Interest rates are prices. In fact, they are the most consequential prices in a market economy because they discount future cash flows and they help us to set investment hurdles and to measure financial risks. In short, interest rates are prices and prices convey information and distorted prices convey misinformation. So if you want to build a factory or estimate the financial risk in a given security today’s interest rates are just the information you don’t need to make an informed decision.”1

Stripping the bearish sentiment (Grant feels Central Banks have targeted low rates for too long now, effectively overheating markets and leading to illusory asset valuations) out of the above statement, it gives us a succinct view of the magnitude of interest rates in markets, and summarizes many points we have hit on so far.

What’s most notable in today’s market climate is that the Federal Reserve is actively hiking the Fed funds rate.
